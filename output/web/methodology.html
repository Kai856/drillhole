Methodology

Neural field prediction of subsurface geology from real drill hole data in the Adavale Basin, Queensland, Australia.

Key insight: This model is trained exclusively on real drill hole data — 86 wells with logged formation contacts. It learns to predict subsurface geology across the entire 256km × 317km basin from these sparse 1D observations, simulating what a mining or exploration company would experience when using their own drilling data.

1. Data Source

The training data consists of 86 real drill holes extracted from a GeoModeller project of the Adavale Basin. Each well has a collar location (X, Y, Z), total depth, and a geological log recording which formation was encountered at each depth interval.

The wells contain 353 formation intervals across 13 geological formations, ranging from shallow Glendower Formation and Eromanga Basin sediments to deep basement granite. Most wells penetrate only the upper 2–4 formations; only a handful reach the deeper Devonian units (Boree Salt, Cooladdi Dolomite, Bury Limestone).

For ground-truth evaluation, a complete 3D voxel model (512 × 634 × 335, ~109M voxels) built by geologists from all available data is used as reference.

2. Train / Test Split

The 86 wells are split into 66 training wells and 20 holdout test wells (random shuffle, seed=42). The model is trained only on data from the 66 training wells and never sees the 20 test wells during training.

Each well's formation intervals are sampled at 20m vertical spacing (matching the voxel grid resolution) to produce discrete training points. Each point is a 3D coordinate (X, Y, elevation) paired with a formation label.

This simulates the real-world scenario: a company provides their drilling data, the model trains on it, and then predicts at undrilled locations.

3. Architecture

The model is a neural field: a function f(x, y, z) → lithology class that takes a 3D coordinate and predicts the geological formation at that location. The architecture is deeper than typical neural fields to help with the sparse data regime.

  • Fourier positional encoding with 10 frequency bands (2^0 to 2^9). Expands 3 input coordinates into a 63-dimensional vector of sine/cosine components, enabling the network to represent sharp geological boundaries.
  • 8-layer MLP with 256 hidden units per layer, ReLU activations.
  • Skip connection at the mid-layer (layer 4), concatenating the original positional encoding back into the hidden state.
  • Classification head: Linear(256, 13) producing logits for each formation.

4. Training

Training on sparse drill hole data presents unique challenges: the model has only 7,188 training points to learn a function over a volume spanning hundreds of kilometers. Key training decisions:

  • Loss function: Cross-entropy with inverse-frequency class weights. Eromanga Basin (63% of data) is down-weighted; rare formations like Boree Salt (0.9%) are up-weighted.
  • Optimizer: Adam with initial learning rate 1e-3.
  • Scheduler: Cosine annealing over 200 epochs.
  • Batch size: 4,096 (entire dataset fits in ~2 batches).
  • Data augmentation: Small spatial jitter (Gaussian noise, σ=0.005 in normalized space) added to coordinates each epoch. This prevents overfitting to exact well locations and encourages smooth spatial interpolation.
  • Coordinate normalization: All (x, y, z) coordinates normalized to [-1, 1].

5. Results

The model achieves 76.2% accuracy on 20 held-out wells it never saw during training. This is a realistic measure of how well sparse drilling data can predict geology at undrilled locations.

Performance varies dramatically by formation. Large, spatially continuous formations (Eromanga, Glendower) are predicted well. Thin, rare, or spatially complex formations (Boree Salt, Lissoy, Cooladdi) are poorly predicted — the model simply doesn't have enough examples to learn their spatial distribution.

6. Full Volume Comparison

As an additional evaluation, the trained model predicts lithology for all 102M valid voxels in the basin. These predictions are compared against the geologist-interpreted 3D model (which was built using all available data including seismic, outcrop maps, and structural interpretation — far more than just drill holes).

The overall volume accuracy is 43.5%. This is expected: 86 sparse drill holes cannot capture the full complexity of a 3D geological model built from multiple data sources. The model correctly identifies the broad stratigraphy but misses detailed structural features.

7. Inference & Visualization

The best checkpoint predicts lithology for all 109M voxels, producing a full 3D predicted volume. This is subsampled (every 10th voxel) and exported as JSON for the interactive web viewer.

The viewer displays all 86 real drill holes with their logged formations. Training wells are shown with blue collars; holdout test wells with orange collars. Users can select rectangular areas on the basin and reveal the neural field's predictions, seeing what the model infers from sparse drilling data alone.

8. Implications

This experiment demonstrates both the potential and limitations of predicting subsurface geology from sparse drill hole data:

  • Major formations are learnable. The model reliably identifies Eromanga Basin, Galilee Basin, and other large units from their spatial distribution across 66 wells.
  • Rare/thin formations need more data. With only 6–8 wells penetrating Boree Salt or Bury Limestone, the model cannot learn their complex spatial distribution.
  • More wells = better predictions. Adding drilling data, especially in undersampled areas or targeting deeper formations, would significantly improve accuracy.
  • Complementary data matters. The full geological model achieves much better results by incorporating seismic surveys, surface geology, structural interpretation, and other data sources beyond drill holes alone.
